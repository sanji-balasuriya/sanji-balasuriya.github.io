Postgraduate research topics

I have "principal supervisor" status at the University of Adelaide, through which I am able to offer a range of postgraduate projects, each of which has the potential of balancing theoretical, computational and applied aspects in ratios according to the student's interests and experience. Some of these projects are offshoots of collaborations with colleagues elsewhere. An incomplete list of postgraduate research thesis topics I would be interested in offering is:

  • Quantifying and optimising flow within floating liquid micro-marbles used for cell culture (based on a project with Nam-Trung Nguyen, Queensland Micro- and Nanotechnology Centre) and Trent Mattner (University of Adelaide)
  • Data-driven dynamical systems
  • Controlling stable and unstable manifolds in fluid flows
  • Defining and quantifying uncertainty of stable and unstable manifolds under small stochasticity (based on a project with Georg Gottwald, University of Sydney)
  • Assimilating satellite measurements of ocean heights and temperatures to determine velocity corrections to oceanic velocity data (based on a project with Julien Le Sommer and Francesco d'Ovidio, CNRS)
  • Development of theoretical Melnikov techniques for locating flow barriers and assessing transport in three-dimensional unsteady flows
  • Analysis of flow barriers in unsteady turbulent velocity data (based on a project with Nick Ouellette, Stanford University)
  • Development of theoretical methodology for transport enhancement in microfluidic channels, with numerical (computational fluid dynamics) validation (based on a project with Zhilin Li, North Carolina State University)
  • Defining and analysing stable and unstable manifolds in finite-time flows
  • Defining and assessing minimum flux curves and surfaces in flows subject to diffusion (based on a project with Gary Froyland, University of New South Wales)
  • Determining wavespeeds and profiles of travelling waves in reaction-diffusion/ecological systems subject to time-delay

I am also able to supervise postgraduate theses of other topics related to my research expertise; please see my Research and Publications pages. I am happy to speak with such students face-to-face (if that is practicable), or via email, to decide on whether a mutually acceptable postgraduate research problem can be formulated. Prospective students will eventually need to be able to enunciate their research programme in their application for studentship and funding, so please be prepared with some initial thoughts before you talk to me.



Honours projects

I am happy to supervise honours projects in many areas of applied mathematics, with the project taking on theoretical, computational or applied flavours (or a combination) depending on students' interests and training. Prospective students are invited to peruse my webpages to see what areas I am interested in; I am also happy to supervise honours projects in allied areas. I have in the past supervised honours projects (or equivalently, advised "senior seminars" or "senior theses" within the US context) in chaotic mixing, difference equations in genetics, existence and uniqueness in stochastic differential equations, sparse coding, mathematical models for the Eiffel Tower, fractal sets and their connectivity, Solow growth models in economics, Hilbert spaces, the mathematics of rowing, Green's functions, wavelets, stability in delay differential equations, and chaotic behaviour of financial markets. Resources/links for prospective postgraduate students

The relevant qualification for entry into these research postgraduate degrees at the University of Adelaide is normally an excellent honours degree from an Australian university, or an equivalent foreign qualification. Foreign students should note that (i) an Australian honours degree is essentially equivalent to a US Masters degree, and (ii) a PhD in Australia is typically a three-year research-only programme which has no coursework. Therefore, foreign students entering the PhD programme at Adelaide usually have at least a Masters-level qualification. An alternative research degree is the two-year coursework+research MPhil degree, which might be more appropriate for foreign students holding only a Bachelors degree.

Domestic students may qualify for funding through Australian Postgraduate Awards, while international students will need to pursue other options for funding. Please explore the above links to determine the possible sources of funding/scholarships, and to find information on the applications process and funding. If applying for scholarships, prospective students will need to prepare their research statement in conjunction with their supervisor.
